Research

My ongoing research coalesces around two main themes.

Economic Insecurity

The first of these is economic insecurity and the political economy of party preferences in Britain. My work with colleagues at the Nuffield Politics Research Centre on the Economic Insecurity: Finding Answers and Solutions project, in collaboration with the Joseph Rowntree Foundation, seeks to understand the electoral consequences of the UK's cost-of-living crisis.

This project has so far resulted in two reports (the most recent of which is available here) and our findings have featured in several news outlets, including BBC Newscast, LBC and The Guardian.

Our ongoing research in this area focuses both on the cost-of-living crisis and broader economic transformations in Britain:

  • Robinson, J., Green, J., Grant, Z., & Evans, G. From the High Street to the Ballot Box: The Political and Attitudinal Consequences of Local Shop Closures.
  • Robinson, J., Green, J., Grant, Z., & Evans, G. A Plague on Both Your Houses: How Anti-Incumbent Economic Voting Punishes Parties in Government and Opposition.
  • Green, J., Robinson, J., Grant, Z., & Evans, G. When the National Economy Gets Personal: Personal Economic Hardship Shapes the Importance of the National Economy to Voters.
  • Evans, G., Grant, Z., Robinson, J., & Green, J. Why AI and Routine Task Automation Have Different Political Implications: The Role of Class, Education and Income.

Political Socialization

The second theme of my research is political socialization, with a particular focus on geographic context. Ongoing research examines how neighbourhoods influence the political preferences of their residents, and how differing residential contexts produce geographic divides in attitudes across electorates.

Current projects include:

  • Robinson, J., Vasilopoulos, P., & McAvay, H. The Enduring Relationship Between Exposure to Immigrants in Adolescence and Support for Immigration in Adulthood.
  • Robinson, J., & Simon, E. Where Are All the Countryside Cosmopolitans? Understanding How Contextual and Compositional Effects Structure the Geography of Immigration Attitudes in Britain.
